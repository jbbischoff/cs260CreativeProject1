Grammar

Unit 4 Grammar

Grammar 4.2

  • Use an adverb to modify or tell more about a verb

  • Use an adverb to modify an adjective or another adverb

  • Use the adverb not to make a sentence negative

  • To compare two actions, add -er to an adverb

  • For adverbs that end in -ly use more or less, most or least

  • To compare three or more actions, add -est to an adverb

  • Some comparison adverbs have irregular forms

  • An adverb that modifies an adjective or another adverb is usually placed before the word it modifies

Grammar 4.1

  • Usually an ADJECTIVE comes before a noun it tells about.

  • A PREDICATE ADJECTIVE appears after a form of be, or another linking verb like seems, appears, or feels.

  • Use an INDEFINITE ADJECTIVE for unknown amounts.

    • Use many or few before a count noun.

    • Use much or less before a noncount noun.

  • To compare two nouns, add -er to an adjective. You will often use the word than in your sentence, too.

  • For adjectives with three or more syllables, use more or less.

  • To compare more than two nouns, add -est to an adjective. Always use the before the adjective.

  • For adjectives with three or more syllables, use most or least

Unit 3 Grammar

Grammar 3.3

  • A pronoun and a verb can be joined to form a contraction.

  • An apostrophe takes the place of the letter or letters that are left out

  • Place not after a form of the verb be.

  • For the future-progressive tense, place not between will and be.

Grammar 3.2

  • The past-progressive tense tells about an action that was happening over a period of time in the past

  • It uses the auxiliary verb was or were plus a main verb that ends in -ing.

  • The auxiliary verb must agree with the subject

  • The future-progressive tense tells about an action that will be happening in the future.

  • It uses the auxiliary verbs will be and a main verb that ends in -ing.

Grammar 3.1

  • An action verb can have two parts: an auxiliary verb and a main verb

  • the auxiliary verb must agree with the subject

  • Auxiliary verbs can be used to form contractions. A pronoun can be joined with an auxiliary verb, or an auxiliary verb can be joined with not.

  • The present-progressive tense tells about an action as it is happening.

  • The present-progressive tense uses the auxiliary verb am, is, or are

  • The main verb ends in -ing.

  • Use not to make some sentences negative.

  • Place not between the auxiliary verb and the main verb. (ex: We are not reading.)

Unit 2 Grammar

Grammar 2.3

  • A possessive noun tells who owns something.

  • For one owner, add ‘s to the singular noun.

  • For more than one owner, just add the apostrophe (‘) to a regular plural noun.

  • For plural nouns that have special forms, add ‘s to the plural noun.

Grammar 2.2

  • The simple subject is whom or what the sentence is about. A noun can be the subject.

  • A direct object receives the action in a sentence. A noun can be the object.

  • Many sentences follow this pattern: Simple subject –> verb –> object

  • Singular object pronouns: me, you, him, her it

  • Plural object pronouns: us, you, them

  • Object pronouns come after action verbs or prepositions, such as: to, for, of

  • Object pronouns agree in number and gender with their antecedents.

Grammar 2.1

  • The subject pronoun must agree in number with its antecedent.

  • Singular subject pronouns: I, you, he, she, it

  • Plural subject pronouns: we, you, they

Unit 1 Grammar

Grammar 1.4

  • A compound subject has two or more subjects joined by the conjunction and or or

  • If the simple subjects in a compound subject are joined by and, use a plural verb.

  • If the simple subjects in a compound subject are joined by or, look at the last simple subject. If it is singular, use a singular verb. If it is plural, use a plural verb.

  • A compound predicate has two or more verbs joined by the conjunction and or or.

  • Every verb in a compound predicate must agree with the subject.

Grammar 1.3

  • A singular subject takes a singular action verb or the singular form of be. (He/She/It take a singular action verb or the singular form of be.)

  • A plural subject takes a plural action verb or the plural form of be. (We/They take a plural action verb or the plural form of be.)

  • I and you always take a plural action verb.

  • I takes a special form of be. You takes the plural form of be.

Grammar 1.2

  • Every sentence must have a subject and a predicate.

  • The simple subject is the noun or pronoun that names who or what the sentence is about.

  • The simple predicate is the verb that tells what someone or something is, does, or has.

  • The complete subject includes all the words that tell about the subject.

  • The complete predicate includes all the words that tell about the predicate. The most important word in the predicate is the verb.

Grammar 1.1

  • Count nouns name things that you can count

  • A singular count noun names one thing.

  • A plural count noun names more than one thing.

  • To make most nouns plural, add -s.

  • For nouns that end in s, z, sh, ch, x, and sometimes o, add -es.

  • If a noun ends in y after a consonant, change the y to i and add -es.

  • If a noun ends in y after a vowel, just add -s.

  • For many nouns that end in f or fe, drop the f or fe and add -ves.

  • For some nouns that end in f or fe, just add -s.

  • Some irregular nouns have special forms.

  • Other irregular nouns have special plural forms.

  • A noncount noun names something you cannot count. It always takes a singular verb.

  • A collective noun names a group of individuals. It is usually singular and takes a singular verb.
