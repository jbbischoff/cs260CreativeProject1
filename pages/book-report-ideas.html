Book Report Ideas

Book reports shouldn’t be a huge project. It can take many different shapes, so make it fun! Here are some creative ideas:

  • Write a book review like you would find in a newspaper.

  • Interview a character from your book and write out the questions and answers.

  • Write a diary entry that the story’s main character might have written either before, during, or after the book’s events.

  • Create a prop or object that is important in the book.

  • Create a short video about the book, using friends as actors.

  • Build a shadow box of a scene in the book. Include an explanation of the scene.

  • Write a letter to one of the book’s characters.

  • Write a paper comparing/contrasting the book with the movie version. It must have been a book before it was a movie.

  • Create a cereal box for the book you read, with a cover, facts about it, and a summary.

  • Make a power point slide show for the book, discussing the main plot or the characters.

  • Create a mini comic book for one of the chapters or scenes of the book.

  • Be a radio reporter and give a report of a scene from the book as if it is happening “live”.

  • Make a mobile with items crucial to the story and write a paragraph explaining your choices.

  • Write about what you learned from the story.

  • Write an alternate beginning or ending to your story.

  • Make a food from the story, or decorate a cake like the cover of the book.

  • Write a poem about the story and a paragraph explaining your choices.

  • Write a letter to a friend about the book, discussing whether you liked it or not and why. (Or to the author).

  • Write the first paragraph (or two) for a sequel and outline what would happen in the rest of the book.

  • Make a poster advertising your book.

  • Write five questions you could ask to see if someone else has read the book. Include the correct answers.

  • Make a collage for the book, or a scene in the book.

  • Make a model of a character, scene, or setting in the book.

  • Make a “wanted” poster for one of the book characters including a drawing, physical description, other important information, and the reward amount.

You do not have to hold yourself to any of the items on this list – be creative! The important thing is that my students are reading.

Book reports should include at least a paragraph of writing (more if you choose a writing based creative project), and will include a short (approximately a minute) pitch to the class describing the book and either recommending the book or expressing why the student disliked it.
